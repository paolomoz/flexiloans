Conclusion

Working capital is the difference between a company’s current assets and liabilities, crucial for handling short-term cash requirements. A working capital policy is a set of guidelines for managing this balance efficiently, determining suitable ratios between liabilities and assets. It aims to balance liquidity, profitability, and risk based on financial objectives, operational cycles, industry dynamics, and risk tolerance. Adequate liquidity is essential for continuous operations to maximize profitability and reduce financial risk. This article discusses different working capital policies and their impact on business loan eligibility.

1. Conservative Working Capital Policy

Companies following a conservative working capital policy maintain more assets, like cash, inventory, and accounts receivable, than current liabilities. By making sure the business has enough cash on hand to meet its short-term obligations even in the face of unanticipated disruptions or bad economic conditions, this strategy places a premium on liquidity and attempts to reduce the risk of financial crisis.

Disadvantages of Conservative Working Capital Policy

  1. Keeping excess liquidity on hand has various disadvantages, although it acts as a safety net against potential cash flow and financial emergencies. One of the major issues arises is the opportunity cost associated with idle funds. Withholding excessive amounts of current assets, instead of investing surplus cash results in lower returns on investment due to un-utilised surplus.
  2. Maintaining high levels of inventory incurs holding costs including insurance premiums, storage fees, and the possibility of spoilage or obsolescence. Similar to this, giving consumers generous credit terms in an effort to increase sales and accounts receivable might divert funds, which, otherwise would have turned into a income generating asset.
  3. Excessive liquidity may signal stakeholders and investors of inefficiency or underutilization of resources, which could have an impact on the company’s valuation and ability to accessibility to capital markets and even business loan.

2. Aggressive Working Capital Policy

Companies following aggressive working capital policy model focus on maintaining lower levels of current assets in comparison to current liabilities. By cutting back on idle cash, this approach seeks to increase profitability, optimise resource usage and increase inventory turnover as well as receivables collection.

One of its key advantages is the ability to increase ROI by allocating excess capital to opportunities with better yields. Businesses can enhance their cash flow and profitability metrics by reducing idle cash and effectively managing inventory levels and receivables collection.

Disadvantages of Aggressive Working Capital Policy

  1. Although this strategy can maximize returns on investment, it may also raise the danger of liquidity constraints and the requirement for outside funding during times of high demand or unstable economic conditions. In such circumstances, the companies might turn to outside funding sources, like short term business loans or other lines of credit.
  2. Stringent credit guidelines or low inventory levels could be detrimental to your suppliers’ and customers’ relationships. Due to inventory restrictions, suppliers could be hesitant to provide consumers favourable loans for businesses credit terms or to give delivery schedules priority, and customers might encounter delays or shortages.
  3. Improper working capital management can lead to operational inefficiencies, disruptions in cash flow, and eventually worse financial performance.

3. Moderate Working Capital Policy

A moderate working capital policy is a well-rounded strategy that aims to maximise current asset levels while taking profitability and liquidity concerns into account. A moderate strategy seeks to achieve a balance between both extremes, compared to the aggressive policies that is focused on decreasing current assets to maximise returns, or conservative policies, which place a premium on keeping high levels of current assets for safety.

Moderate working capital policy is best for companies seeking to maintain current assets at levels that effectively support their daily operations without tying up excessive capital. This requires preserving ideal profitability to liquidity ratio. This policy allows flexibility to have enough resources to meet short-term obligations and maximise returns on investment by carefully controlling the composition, utilisation of current assets capitalize on growth opportunities without exposing themselves to excessive risk..

Disadvantages of Moderate Working Capital Policy

  1. To avoid potential pitfalls, it is imperative that companies regularly monitor and manage their working capital levels.
  2. Excessive inventory holding and extended loan for business terms can increase holding costs and tie up capital.
  3. Poor liquidity might make the company more susceptible to cash flow disruptions or solvency problems.

4. Permanent Working Capital Policy

Permanent working capital policy is the method when a company maintains a level of assets in a continuum to  support its ongoing operations and sales activities. Permanent working capital remains relatively constant over time than temporary working capital, which fluctuates with seasonal or cyclical variations in business activities. This policy guarantees that the business has enough cash on hand to cover monthly expenses, finance daily operations, and sustain the kind of sales that are anticipated under regular company conditions.

This policy is at the core of a company’s operational functions like maintaining inventory levels, funding accounts receivable, and covering operating expenses. One of its key characteristics is its stability and predictability and given its long-term characteristic, it requires long-term loan for business.

Disadvantages of Permanent Working Capital Policy

  1. Retaining a suitable quantity of fixed capital is crucial in order to reduce the risk of liquidity and ensure the financial stability of any organisation. Inadequate permanent working capital can result in financial commitments not being met, liquidity issues, and interruptions in business operations.
  2. Excessive permanent working capital poses the risk of less profitable and inefficient use of resources. Insufficient management may strain cash-flow which, in turn affects a company’s ability for eligibility of business loans.
  3. Accurate projections of upcoming sales, costs, and operating requirements are necessary to determine the ideal amount of permanent working capital. Mismatch between actual and predicted working capital demands might result in either excess or insufficient liquidity due to forecasting errors or unanticipated changes in market conditions.

How Working Capital Polices affect a Company’s Business Loan Capabilities

Working capital policy are an important indicator that affects a company’s eligibility for business loans. Different policies allow for different kind of flexibility or possible constraints. Following are some pointers that reflect how a working capital policy can influence credit eligibility of a company:

  1. Liquidity Position: For any lender, when assessing a company’s ability to fulfil their immediate financial obligations, the lenders evaluate their liquidity position. The conservative working capital, that maintains higher levels of current assets relative to liability, may indicate excellent liquidity level and financial stability. This increases a company’s creditworthiness and chances to get approved faster when they apply for business loan.
  2. The Risk Factor: The risk profile of any company is a crucial criterion of evaluation for any lender. A more aggressive working capital policy that keeps current asset levels lower could be a sign of tighter liquidity and pose a greater appetite for risk. Although the method is aims to maximise resource usability to generate profitability, it could be a red signal for lenders for eligibility of business loan, raising concerns about the company’s capacity to overcome challenges and ability make timely loan repayments.
  3. Cash-Flow: A company’s working capital policy affects the cash conversion cycle, receivables collection, and inventory turnover are all impacted by a company’s working capital policy. Creditors look at these things to determine whether the business can make enough to repay the loan. The moderate working capital policy can enhance a company’s business loan eligibility by maintaining a balance between profitability and liquidity. This policy is also a good indicator of sound cash flow management techniques.
  4. Collateral Bidding: Collateral may be necessary for financial institutions in order to reduce their lending risk. Lenders may accept different kinds and values of collateral depending on the working capital policies of a company. Businesses that have conservative policies and substantial current assets to offer as collateral may be able to secure business loans ease without much restrictions. 
  5. Ratio Assessment: Sometimes, it is the game of ratios. Creditors and NBFCs may assess your financial ratios, such as the debt-to-equity ratio, quick ratio, and current ratio, to evaluate the solvency and repayment capacity of your company. By dictating the makeup of current assets and liabilities, the working capital policy has a direct effect on these ratios. Companies that maintain a healthy balance between their leverage and liquidity ratios and have well-managed working capital practices have a higher chance of meeting the requirements set by creditors for business loan approval.
